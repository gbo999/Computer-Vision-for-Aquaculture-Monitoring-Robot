The Technology Pipeline: From Video to Measurement

To overcome these challenges, a novel system was developed. It uses a mobile camera platform and a sophisticated computer vision pipeline to automate the entire measurement process, making it non-invasive, scalable, and highly accurate.

Validation & Accuracy: Putting the System to the Test

Accuracy vs. Manual Measurements

The system's automated measurements were compared against repeated manual measurements by an expert. This chart shows the Mean Absolute Error (MAE) in millimeters for different pond environments.

Lower MAE indicates higher accuracy. The system's carapace length error (~3mm) is impressively close to the variability of human experts.

Accuracy vs. Known Physical Size

To test against a true ground truth, prawn exuviae (molted shells) of known lengths were measured. This chart shows the Mean Absolute Relative Error (MARE) for this validation.

The system achieved a relative error between 3.3% and 6.4%, demonstrating strong performance in a realistic setting.

Dissecting the Error: What Causes Inaccuracy?

When errors over 5% occurred, they were analyzed to find the root cause. Was it the pixel-to-mm conversion (Scale Error) or the AI's ability to place keypoints (Pixel Error)? The results confirm the robustness of the measurement methodology.

As shown, the vast majority of significant errors stem from Pixel Error. This refers to slight inaccuracies in the AI's placement of anatomical keypoints. In contrast, Scale Error, which relates to the trigonometric conversion from pixels to millimeters, was a much smaller factor.


This is a critical finding: it means the core measurement calculation is sound. Future improvements in model accuracy and training data will directly reduce the primary source of error.
